# A Kamala weekend

## Subtitle / dek
Three days. Held carefully. Slower than you expect.

## The shape of it

Every Kamala retreat follows the same arc, even as the venues and practitioners change. Friday softens you. Saturday opens you. Sunday returns you, gently, to the world.

What follows is the rhythm of a typical weekend. The detailed schedule for any given retreat lives on its own page.

## Friday — arrival and softening

You arrive at seven. The drive in is quiet, lined with trees. Someone meets you at the door with something warm to drink.

Your room is ready. There's a welcome pack on the bed, an itinerary you can read or not read, and time to unpack at your own pace. Nobody is rushing you.

At eight, we eat together. Family-style, around one long table, seasonal food cooked with care. This is the first time you'll meet the others. The conversation finds its own level.

After dinner, we gather by candlelight for cacao. Ceremonial-grade, warm, slightly bitter. There's gentle breathwork, and then a journal in your hands and two questions:

*What do I want to leave behind this weekend?*
*What do I want to take home?*

You write what comes. We close with a quiet collective intention, and the lights go down by ten. Chamomile and valerian if you want them. Sleep finds most people quickly.

## Saturday — release

You wake when you wake. The drinks station is open from early — matcha, lions mane coffee, pressed juices, turmeric lattes, herbal teas, antioxidant shots, coconut water. Take what you need.

Breakfast at nine, generous and unhurried. After, the MC sets the tone for the day in fifteen minutes flat. No lectures. Just enough to orient you.

At half past ten, the gong meditation begins. Ninety minutes of gentle movement, short breathwork, then lying down under blankets while gongs, chimes and sacred sound move through the room. There are no words. Most people drift between rest and something deeper.

The middle of the day is a graze — food laid out, eaten when you're ready.

Then comes the heart of the weekend. Two and a half hours of somatic breathwork and guided movement. This is where the work tends to live. It's deep, it can be intense, and it's held safely by practitioners who know how to hold it. What surfaces, surfaces. What needs to move, moves.

Afterwards, the afternoon is yours. Three hours of nothing scheduled. Walk the grounds. Sit by the fire pit. Sleep. Read. Play chess badly. The cinema room is open with curated documentaries. The drinks station is still going.

Dinner at seven, candlelit, the menu shaped by what the day asked for. Quieter than Friday. The room has changed.

At half past eight we close the day with reishi tea — the mushroom of calm — short breathwork, and the journal again:

*What shifted today? What surprised me? What feels different in my body from yesterday?*

Lights down at ten. From here on, silence.

## Sunday — float

Yoga at eight, optional but worth attending. Sixty minutes of restorative shapes, just enough to wake the body without asking too much.

Breakfast at nine. The last meal as a full group.

At ten, we move to the pool. Twelve metres of warm indoor water, the spa complex held for the group only. You float — towels, robes, flotation aids all provided — while a sound healer works at the water's edge. Bowls, voice, breath. The water carries you. Most people don't want to leave.

After, the spa is open until half past two. Pool, jacuzzi, sauna, steam room. If you booked the in-room massage, it comes to you. There's no pressure to do anything in particular.

At one, lunch — a farewell graze, light and bright.

At two we close. Each guest leaves with their journal, a Kamala reishi set, and a pressed juice for the road. Latest checkout is three.

You drive back into your life slower than you arrived.
